# Unidiomatic English

Load this when copy is grammatical and free of obvious AI tells but still does not
read the way a fluent native writer would put it. This is a distinct failure class
from "AI slop." A calque or a stiff collocation can come from translation, from a
non-native draft, or from a model averaging over too many registers. The words are
correct; the combination is not what a fluent writer reaches for.

Core test: is each word individually fine but the pairing something a native writer
would not actually say? If so, it is unidiomatic, and the fix is the idiom, not a
synonym.

## 31. Collocation errors
Tell: two words that are each correct but do not go together in fluent usage.

| Unidiomatic | Idiomatic |
|---|---|
| make a decision about whether to | decide whether to |
| do a mistake | make a mistake |
| say an opinion | give an opinion / say what you think |
| heavy traffic of users | heavy traffic / a lot of users |
| close attention to the details | close attention to detail |
| make a research | do research / run a study |
| take a decision (US copy) | make a decision |
| strong rain | heavy rain |
| fast food restaurant chain leader | the biggest fast-food chain |

Fix: replace the whole pairing with the collocation a native speaker uses. Do not
swap one half for a synonym; that usually keeps it unidiomatic.

## 32. Calques and translated-from-nowhere phrasing
Tell: structures that map cleanly onto another language but are stilted in English,
or sentences with no idiomatic anchor at all, as if translated from a language that
was never specified.

Watch: "since ever," "in the same time," "for to do," "informations," "the most of,"
"persons" (for people), "more better," "according to me," "I will explain you,"
"discuss about," "comprised of" (use "composed of" or "comprises").

Before:
> According to me, the most of users will discuss about the new feature since ever.

After:
> I think most users will talk about the new feature for a long time.

## 33. Over-Latinate diction where a plain word fits
Tell: a long Latin-derived word chosen over the short native one a fluent writer
would use in running prose. Common in copy that is trying to sound authoritative.

| Inflated | Plain |
|---|---|
| utilize | use |
| facilitate | help / make easier |
| commence | start |
| endeavor | try |
| sufficient | enough |
| numerous | many |
| obtain | get |
| in the vicinity of | near / about |
| at the present juncture | now |
| subsequent to | after |

Keep the Latinate word only when it carries a precise sense the plain word loses
(for example, "utilize" meaning to put to a use other than the intended one, or
domain terms like "facilitate" in a workshop context).

## 34. Register mismatch
Tell: a single sentence mixes formal and casual diction, or the register does not
fit the channel (legal boilerplate in a friendly email, slang in a board memo).

Before:
> Pursuant to our conversation, the synergies are gonna be huge and we'll circle back to action the deliverables.

After (formal channel):
> Following our conversation, we will confirm the next steps and the expected benefits by Friday.

After (casual channel):
> Following up on our chat: I'll send the next steps and what we expect to get out of it by Friday.

Fix: pick the register the channel and relationship call for, then make every clause
match it. See `voice-and-register.md` for the per-channel guidance.

## 35. Preposition and particle misuse
Tell: the wrong preposition or a missing/extra particle. Individually small, but they
are the fastest way a reader senses a non-native or machine hand.

| Unidiomatic | Idiomatic |
|---|---|
| discuss about the plan | discuss the plan |
| explain me the process | explain the process to me |
| depends of the budget | depends on the budget |
| different than last year | different from last year |
| comprised of three parts | composed of three parts / comprises three parts |
| in the other hand | on the other hand |
| married with | married to |
| good in math | good at math |

## 36. Article and number agreement drift
Tell: missing or extra articles, or mass nouns treated as count nouns.

Watch: "an information," "informations," "a feedback," "feedbacks," "an advice,"
"advices," "researches" (as plural of research), "softwares," "equipments."

Before:
> She gave me an useful feedback and two advices about the softwares.

After:
> She gave me useful feedback and two pieces of advice about the software.

## Rewrite heuristic

For each flagged phrase, ask: what is the fixed expression a fluent writer uses for
this exact meaning? Replace the whole phrase with that expression. If you are not
certain an English idiom exists for the intended meaning, keep the author's meaning
and choose the plainest literal phrasing, rather than guessing at an idiom that may
not fit. Never "correct" deliberate dialect, character voice, or a quoted speaker.
