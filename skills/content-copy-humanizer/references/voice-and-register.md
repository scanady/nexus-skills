# Voice and Register

Removing AI tells is half the job. Sterile, voiceless prose is just as obviously
synthetic as slop, because no real person writes with zero point of view. But the
fix is not "add personality." The fix is to add the voice the channel calls for.
A breezy first-person aside that saves a blog post will sink an executive memo.

Use this reference to calibrate. Match the register first, then add a pulse inside it.

## Read the register before you touch the voice

| Channel | Voice it wants | Add | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Technical doc / API reference | Calm, precise, second person or imperative | Confident direct statements, worked examples | Jokes, "I keep thinking about," rhetorical questions, hype adjectives |
| Executive memo / board update | Decisive, owns a recommendation | A clear stance, the number that matters, the tradeoff | Hedging stacks, breezy asides, throat-clearing, suspense |
| Brand / marketing site | Specific, concrete, can have edge | A real claim, a named proof point, a distinct angle | Faux-profound abstraction, mechanism-free promises, adjective piles |
| Blog / personal essay / newsletter | First person, opinionated, varied rhythm | Reactions, mixed feelings, a tangent, an admission of doubt | Press-release neutrality, uniform sentence length |
| Email | Matches the relationship and the ask | Plain direct sentences, one clear request | Corporate boilerplate to a peer, slang to a stranger |
| Academic / report | Measured, evidence-led | Precise claims, named sources, honest limitations | Promotional diction, rule-of-three flourishes, generic upbeat endings |

When you do not know the channel, ask or infer from the source. Do not default to
the blog voice; it is the narrowest fit of the six.

## Signs of soulless writing (clean but dead)

- Every sentence is the same length and shape.
- Pure neutral reporting with no stance, where a stance is expected.
- No acknowledgment of uncertainty, cost, or mixed evidence.
- No first person where the channel invites it.
- Reads like a press release or a Wikipedia stub regardless of context.

## How to add a pulse, inside the register

Have a point of view. React to facts, do not only list them. In a memo this is a
recommendation; in an essay it is an opinion; in a brand line it is a specific
claim. Neutral everywhere reads as machine-written.

Vary the rhythm. Short sentence. Then a longer one that takes its time and earns the
length. Uniform cadence is the most reliable sign of generated prose.

Be concrete about stakes and feelings. Not "this is concerning" but the specific
thing that is concerning and to whom. Specificity is what reads as human; vague
emotion reads as synthetic empathy.

Let a little mess in, where the channel allows it. A tangent, an aside, an admitted
doubt. A blog tolerates this; a board memo tolerates one well-placed aside; an API
doc tolerates none.

Use the first person when the channel invites it and the author would. "I" is honest
in an essay, an email, or a newsletter. It is usually wrong in a spec or a policy.

## Worked contrast: same facts, three registers

Source facts: an agent run generated 3 million lines of code overnight; reactions
were split; impact is unclear.

Soulless (any channel):
> The experiment produced interesting results. The agents generated 3 million lines of code. Some developers were impressed while others were skeptical. The implications remain unclear.

Blog / newsletter:
> 3 million lines of code, generated while everyone slept. Half the dev world is losing it, half are explaining why it does not count. The honest answer is probably somewhere boring in the middle, but I keep coming back to those agents grinding away at 3am with nobody watching.

Executive update:
> Overnight, the agents produced 3 million lines of code. We do not yet know how much is usable; early review suggests most needs rework. Recommendation: fund a two-week audit before we draw conclusions, rather than scaling the run.

Each has a pulse. Only one of them belongs in a board deck.

## Guardrail

Do not flatten a voice that is already specific, contextual, and human, even if it
breaks a "rule." Deliberate fragments, a well-placed em dash in genuinely literary
prose, a repeated phrase used for rhythm: these are choices, not tells. The target
is synthetic flatness and misfit register, not every departure from plain style.
