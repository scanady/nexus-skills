# Persuasion & Pricing Psychology

Influence frameworks and pricing mental models for ethical marketing. Covers Cialdini principles, prospect theory, and pricing perception.

---

## Reciprocity Principle

People feel obligated to return favors. Give first → they want to give back.

**Marketing use**: Free content, free tools, generous free tiers create reciprocal obligation. Give value before asking for anything. Lead magnets work because of reciprocity, not just gating.

---

## Commitment & Consistency

Once people commit to something, they stay consistent with that commitment. Small yeses lead to big yeses.

**Marketing use**: Get small commitments first — email signup, free trial, quiz completion. People who took one step more likely to take next. Micro-conversions build toward macro-conversions.

---

## Authority Bias

People defer to experts and authority figures. Credentials and expertise create trust shortcuts.

**Marketing use**: Feature expert endorsements, certifications, "featured in" logos, thought leadership content. Third-party validation > self-promotion. "Recommended by 9 out of 10 dentists" works for reason.

---

## Liking / Similarity Bias

People say yes to those they like and those similar to themselves. Relatability drives persuasion.

**Marketing use**: Relatable spokespeople, founder stories, community language. "Built by marketers for marketers" signals similarity. Mirror your audience's identity and values.

---

## Unity Principle

Shared identity drives influence. "One of us" more powerful than "someone I like."

**Marketing use**: Position brand as part of customer's tribe. Insider language, shared values, community membership. "Fellow founders" hits harder than "our customers."

---

## Scarcity / Urgency

Limited availability increases perceived value. Scarcity signals desirability and triggers fear of missing out.

**Marketing use**: Limited-time offers, low-stock warnings, exclusive access create urgency. Only use when genuine — fake scarcity destroys trust fast. "3 spots left" must be real.

**Ethics flag**: Artificial scarcity = dark pattern. Countdown timers that reset = manipulation. Use only genuine constraints.

---

## Foot-in-the-Door

Start small request, then escalate. Compliance with small request leads to compliance with larger one.

**Marketing use**: Free trial → paid plan → annual plan → enterprise. Each step builds on last. Newsletter signup → webinar → demo → purchase. Ladder of engagement.

---

## Door-in-the-Face

Start with unreasonably large request, retreat to actual ask. Contrast makes second request seem reasonable.

**Marketing use**: Show enterprise pricing first, then reveal affordable starter plan. Contrast makes it feel like deal. "Custom pricing starts at $50K... or get started at $49/month."

---

## Loss Aversion / Prospect Theory

Losses feel roughly 2x as painful as equivalent gains feel good. People work harder to avoid losing than to gain.

**Marketing use**: Frame in terms of what they lose by not acting. "Don't miss out" beats "You could gain." "Stop losing $X/month" stronger than "Save $X/month." Show cost of inaction.

**Ethics flag**: Loss framing can cross into fear manipulation. Use to highlight genuine opportunity cost, not manufactured panic.

---

## Anchoring Effect

First number seen heavily influences all subsequent judgments. Anchor sets reference frame.

**Marketing use**: Show higher price first — original price, competitor price, enterprise tier. "$299 value, yours for $49" sets anchor at $299. First number wins.

---

## Decoy Effect

Adding third, inferior option makes one original option look better. Asymmetric dominance shifts preference.

**Marketing use**: "Decoy" pricing tier that's clearly worse value makes preferred tier obvious choice. Magazine: digital $59, print $125, print+digital $125. Print-only is decoy making bundle look brilliant.

---

## Framing Effect

How something presented changes perception. Same facts, different frames → different decisions.

**Marketing use**: "90% success rate" vs "10% failure rate" — identical but feel different. "Save 5 hours/week" vs "Reclaim a half day every week." Choose frame that resonates with audience motivation.

---

## Contrast Effect

Things seem different depending on comparison context. Perception is relative, not absolute.

**Marketing use**: Show "before" state clearly. Contrast with "after" makes improvements vivid. Show competitor experience, then yours. Juxtaposition amplifies perceived difference.

---

## Charm Pricing / Left-Digit Effect

Prices ending in 9 seem significantly lower. $99 feels much cheaper than $100. Left digit dominates.

**Marketing use**: Use .99 or .95 endings for value-focused products. Works because brain encodes left digit first. $99 encoded as "ninety-something," $100 as "one hundred."

---

## Rounded-Price (Fluency) Effect

Round numbers feel premium, easier to process. $100 signals quality; $99 signals value.

**Marketing use**: Round prices for premium products ($500/month). Charm prices for value products ($497/month). Match price format to positioning.

---

## Rule of 100

Under $100: percentage discounts feel larger ("20% off"). Over $100: absolute discounts feel larger ("$50 off"). Same discount, different perception.

**Marketing use**: $80 product: "20% off" beats "$16 off." $500 product: "$100 off" beats "20% off." Pick format that makes number feel bigger.

---

## Price Relativity / Good-Better-Best

People judge prices relative to options presented. Middle tier seems reasonable between cheap and expensive.

**Marketing use**: Three tiers where middle is target. Expensive tier makes middle look reasonable. Cheap tier provides anchor and serves price-sensitive segment. Most buyers pick middle.

---

## Mental Accounting (Pricing)

Same price framed differently changes perception. Daily cost feels smaller than monthly lump sum.

**Marketing use**: "$1/day" feels cheaper than "$30/month." "Less than your morning coffee" reframes expense into acceptable mental category. Break big numbers into small familiar ones.

## Quick Reference

| Model | Best For |
|-------|---------|
| Reciprocity | Lead generation, content strategy |

| Decoy Effect | Pricing tier optimization |

| Good-Better-Best | Pricing architecture |

## Ethics Checklist for Persuasion Tactics

Before deploying influence tactic, verify:

- [ ] Scarcity is genuine (not manufactured countdowns)
- [ ] Loss framing highlights real cost, not manufactured fear
- [ ] Social proof uses real numbers and testimonials
- [ ] Defaults serve customer interest, not just revenue
- [ ] Customer can easily reverse decision (cancel, refund)
- [ ] Tactic works long-term — doesn't erode trust on second encounter
