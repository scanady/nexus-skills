# Buyer Psychology & Decision-Making

Mental models explaining how customers think, decide, and behave. Use for understanding purchase barriers, retention drivers, and cognitive patterns.

---

## Fundamental Attribution Error

People blame character for others' behavior, ignore circumstances. "They didn't buy because they're not serious" vs "checkout was confusing."

**Marketing use**: When customers don't convert, examine your process before blaming them. Problem usually situational — bad UX, unclear value prop, too much friction — not personal.

---

## Mere Exposure Effect

Familiarity breeds liking. People prefer things they've encountered before, even without conscious memory.

**Marketing use**: Consistent brand presence builds preference over time. Repetition across channels creates comfort and trust. Show up reliably. Brand recall > single brilliant ad.

---

## Availability Heuristic

People judge likelihood by how easily examples come to mind. Recent, vivid events seem more common than they are.

**Marketing use**: Case studies and testimonials make success feel achievable. Make positive outcomes easy to imagine. "500 companies saved $2M" sticks better than abstract claims.

---

## Confirmation Bias

People seek info confirming existing beliefs, ignore contradictory evidence. Fighting beliefs head-on rarely works.

**Marketing use**: Understand what audience already believes. Align messaging with those beliefs, then expand. Don't argue against their worldview — build from it.

---

## Lindy Effect

Longer something survived, longer it likely continues. Old ideas often outlast new ones.

**Marketing use**: Proven marketing principles (clear value props, social proof) outlast trendy tactics. Don't abandon fundamentals for fads. What worked for decades probably still works.

---

## Mimetic Desire

People want things because others want them. Desire is socially contagious, not individually generated.

**Marketing use**: Show desirable people wanting your product. Waitlists, exclusivity, visible demand trigger mimetic desire. "Sold out" is powerful marketing.

---

## Sunk Cost Fallacy

People continue investing because of past investment, even when irrational. Past spend clouds future decisions.

**Marketing use**: Know when to kill underperforming campaigns. Past spend shouldn't justify future spend if results absent. Also applies to customers — their investment in your product creates stickiness.

---

## Endowment Effect

People value things more once they own them. Ownership changes perception of worth.

**Marketing use**: Free trials, samples, freemium models let customers "own" product. Once they have it, giving it up feels like loss. Trial-to-paid conversion leverages this.

---

## IKEA Effect

People value things more when they helped create them. Effort increases perceived value.

**Marketing use**: Let customers customize, configure, build. Their investment increases commitment. Onboarding that involves setup choices creates ownership feeling.

---

## Zero-Price Effect

Free isn't just low price — it's psychologically different category. Triggers irrational preference.

**Marketing use**: Free tiers, free trials, free shipping have disproportionate appeal. Jump from $1 to $0 bigger than $2 to $1. "Free" removes entire category of decision friction.

---

## Hyperbolic Discounting

People strongly prefer immediate rewards over future ones, even when waiting more rational.

**Marketing use**: Emphasize immediate benefits. "Start saving time today" beats "You'll see ROI in 6 months." Show instant value, not delayed promises.

---

## Status-Quo Bias

People prefer current state. Change requires effort and feels risky. Default = stay put.

**Marketing use**: Reduce friction to switch. Make transition feel safe and easy. "Import your data in one click." Address fear of change directly. Show that switching is less painful than they think.

---

## Default Effect

People accept pre-selected options. Defaults are powerful because choosing different requires active effort.

**Marketing use**: Pre-select plan you want customers to choose. Default annual billing if it's better value. Opt-out structures beat opt-in (applied ethically — user must understand what they're getting).

---

## Paradox of Choice

Too many options overwhelm and paralyze. Fewer choices often lead to more decisions.

**Marketing use**: Limit options. Three pricing tiers beat seven. Recommend single "best for most" option. Remove unnecessary configuration from onboarding.

---

## Goal-Gradient Effect

People accelerate effort as they approach goal. Showing progress motivates action.

**Marketing use**: Progress bars, completion percentages, "almost there" messaging drive completion. Show users how close they are. Works for onboarding, checkout, loyalty programs.

---

## Peak-End Rule

People judge experiences by peak moment (best or worst) and ending, not average of whole experience.

**Marketing use**: Design memorable peaks — surprise upgrades, delightful moments. Nail the ending — thank you pages, follow-up emails, unboxing. Bad ending ruins good experience.

---

## Zeigarnik Effect

Unfinished tasks occupy mind more than completed ones. Open loops create tension that demands resolution.

**Marketing use**: "You're 80% done" creates pull to finish. Incomplete profiles, abandoned cart reminders, cliffhangers all leverage this. Open the loop, then offer resolution.

---

## Pratfall Effect

Competent entities become more likable when they show small flaw. Perfection is less relatable.

**Marketing use**: Admitting weakness ("We're not the cheapest, but...") increases trust and differentiation. Authenticity > polish. Small vulnerability makes brand human.

---

## Curse of Knowledge

Once you know something, can't imagine not knowing it. Experts struggle to explain simply.

**Marketing use**: Your product seems obvious to you but confusing to newcomers. Test copy with people unfamiliar with your space. Jargon that feels natural to you is gibberish to prospects.

---

## Mental Accounting

People treat money differently based on source or intended use, even though money is fungible.

**Marketing use**: Frame costs in favorable mental accounts. "$3/day" feels different than "$90/month" — same amount, different mental bucket. "Less than your morning coffee" reframes expense.

---

## Regret Aversion

People avoid actions that might cause regret, even if expected outcome positive. Fear of wrong choice freezes decisions.

**Marketing use**: Address regret directly. Money-back guarantees, free trials, "no commitment" messaging reduce regret fear. Remove risk from decision.

---

## Bandwagon Effect / Social Proof

People follow what others do. Popularity signals quality and safety. Numbers create confidence.

**Marketing use**: Show customer counts, testimonials, logos, reviews, "trending" indicators. "Join 50,000+ teams" is more persuasive than any feature list. Make popularity visible.
