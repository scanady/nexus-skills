# Strategic Thinking & Growth Models

Mental models for marketing strategy, resource allocation, system-level decisions, and scaling.

---

## First Principles

Break problem to basic truths, build up from there. Stop copying competitors blindly.

**Technique**: Ask "why" 5 times to find root cause. Each answer peels a layer.

**Marketing use**: Don't assume content marketing needed because competitors do it. Ask why you need it, what problem it solves, whether better solution exists. Challenge inherited assumptions.

---

## Jobs to Be Done

People don't buy products — they hire them for a job. Focus on outcome, not features.

**Marketing use**: Drill buyer wants hole, not drill. Frame product around job it accomplishes. "Get invoices paid 3x faster" beats "AI-powered invoicing platform."

---

## Circle of Competence

Know what you're good at. Stay in it. Venture outside only with proper learning or expert help.

**Marketing use**: Don't chase every channel. Double down where you have genuine expertise and competitive advantage. Doing TikTok poorly wastes more than skipping it.

---

## Inversion

Flip question. Instead of "How do I succeed?" ask "What guarantees failure?" Avoid those things.

**Marketing use**: List everything that kills campaign — confusing messaging, wrong audience, slow page, unclear CTA. Systematically prevent each. Sometimes avoiding mistakes beats chasing brilliance.

---

## Occam's Razor

Simplest explanation usually correct. Don't overcomplicate attribution when simple cause exists.

**Marketing use**: Conversions dropped? Check obvious first — broken form, slow page, changed pricing. Before building complex attribution model, verify basics work.

---

## Pareto Principle (80/20)

80% of results from 20% of efforts. Find vital few.

**Marketing use**: Identify 20% of channels, customers, or content driving 80% of results. Cut or reduce rest. One high-performing channel beat five mediocre ones.

---

## Local vs Global Optima

Local optimum = best nearby solution. Global optimum = best overall. Don't optimize wrong thing.

**Marketing use**: Optimizing email subject lines (local) won't help if email wrong channel (global). Zoom out before zooming in. Solve right problem first.

---

## Theory of Constraints

Every system has one bottleneck limiting throughput. Fix that constraint before optimizing elsewhere.

**Marketing use**: Funnel converts well but traffic low? More CRO won't help. Fix traffic bottleneck first. Find where pipeline chokes, fix that one thing.

---

## Opportunity Cost

Every choice costs what you gave up. Compare against alternatives, not just against doing nothing.

**Marketing use**: Time on low-ROI channel = time not spent on high-ROI activities. Always ask "what else could this budget/time achieve?"

---

## Diminishing Returns

Past certain point, more investment yields less gain. Know when curve flattens.

**Marketing use**: 10th blog post won't match first one's impact. Know when to diversify rather than double down. Track marginal return on each additional unit of effort.

---

## Second-Order Thinking

Consider effects of effects. First-order thinking = surface. Second-order = consequences.

**Marketing use**: Flash sale boosts revenue (first order) but trains customers to wait for discounts (second order). Aggressive acquisition might tank retention. Think two steps ahead.

---

## Map ≠ Territory

Models represent reality but aren't reality. Analytics dashboard ≠ actual customer experience.

**Marketing use**: Customer persona is useful model but real customers more complex. Survey data ≠ real behavior. Stay in touch with actual users. Data informs, doesn't replace observation.

---

## Probabilistic Thinking

Think in probabilities, not certainties. Estimate likelihoods, plan for multiple outcomes.

**Marketing use**: Don't bet everything on one campaign. Spread risk. Plan for scenarios where primary strategy underperforms. Portfolio approach beats single bet.

---

## Barbell Strategy

Combine extreme safety with small high-risk/high-reward bets. Avoid mediocre middle.

**Marketing use**: 80% budget into proven channels, 20% into experimental bets. Middle-risk, middle-reward activities waste resources. Go safe or go bold.

---

## Feedback Loops

Output becomes input, creating cycles. Positive loops accelerate; negative loops decline.

**Marketing use**: Build virtuous cycles: more users → more content → better SEO → more users. Identify your loops. Strengthen positive ones, break negative ones.

---

## Compounding

Small consistent gains accumulate into big results over time. Early gains matter most.

**Marketing use**: Content, SEO, brand building compound. Start early. Blog with 200 posts and 3 years of domain authority beats new competitor on day one. Consistency > intensity.

---

## Network Effects

Product becomes more valuable as more people use it. Value scales with adoption.

**Marketing use**: Design features that improve with users — shared workspaces, integrations, marketplaces, communities. Network effects create moats.

---

## Flywheel Effect

Sustained effort creates momentum that eventually maintains itself. Hard to start, easy to maintain once spinning.

**Marketing use**: Content → traffic → leads → customers → case studies → more content. Each element powers next. Push hardest at beginning; momentum compounds.

---

## Switching Costs

Price of changing to competitor — time, money, effort, data. High switching costs = retention.

**Marketing use**: Increase switching costs ethically: integrations, data accumulation, workflow customization, team adoption. Make product more valuable over time, not just harder to leave.

---

## Exploration vs Exploitation

Balance trying new things with optimizing what works.

**Marketing use**: Don't abandon working channels for shiny new ones. But allocate some budget to experiments. 70/20/10: 70% proven, 20% adjacent, 10% experimental.

---

## Critical Mass / Tipping Point

Threshold after which growth becomes self-sustaining.

**Marketing use**: Focus resources on reaching critical mass in one segment before expanding. Depth before breadth. Own one niche completely, then expand.

---

## Survivorship Bias

Focus on successes, ignore invisible failures. Distorts perception of what works.

**Marketing use**: Study failed campaigns, not just successful ones. Viral hit you're copying had 99 failures you didn't see. Sample from failures too.
