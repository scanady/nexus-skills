# Format Guidance

The seven format containers used for executive communication. Each format has conventions about length, structure, scannability, and visual treatment. Most narrative patterns work in most formats, but some combinations are stronger than others.

Use this reference after the objective is diagnosed and the pattern is selected. The pattern determines the substance; the format determines the container.

## 1. Executive Memo

**Purpose:** Decision-oriented document distributed in advance of a meeting, or as a standalone artifact for asynchronous reading. Used when the topic warrants more than an email but does not need a presentation.

**Conventions:**
- One to two pages, three at the absolute outside
- Section headers that carry meaning (not "Background" and "Discussion" but "Why we are not buying" and "What changes if we wait")
- Subject line or title that previews the conclusion: "Recommending Vendor A for the platform consolidation" beats "Platform consolidation analysis"
- Page one carries the recommendation and the ask; supporting detail follows
- Numbers in tables when comparison matters, in prose when context matters

**Soft length target:** 600 to 1,200 words. Longer than this, the reader skims; shorter than this, an email would do.

**Pattern compatibility:**
- Strongest: decision request, strategic narrative, capability or roadmap brief
- Workable: incident or post-mortem, technical explanation
- Awkward: status update (too heavy), change announcement (too formal), reassurance (too cold), escalation (too slow)

**Format-specific guidance:**
- The first line of the memo should be the recommendation or the headline. Not "the purpose of this memo is."
- If there is an executive summary, it must contain the recommendation and the ask. A summary that does not is just an extended subject line.
- Tables earn their place when they show comparison or trade-off. Tables of unrelated items are clutter.
- Footnotes are acceptable for source attribution and definitions; not for argument.

## 2. Executive Email

**Purpose:** Single-topic message that asks for or conveys something specific. Most executive emails fail by trying to do too much.

**Conventions:**
- Subject line states the action or the conclusion: "Need your sign-off on Vendor A by Friday" beats "Vendor decision"
- Body under 200 words ideally, under 300 in any case
- Front-loaded ask if there is one
- No preamble ("I hope this finds you well"), no recap of context the recipient already has
- Bullets for parallel items only; prose for everything else

**Soft length target:** 80 to 200 words.

**Pattern compatibility:**
- Strongest: decision request (when short), escalation, status update (recurring), reassurance, change announcement (short form)
- Workable: technical explanation (when the topic is narrow)
- Awkward: strategic narrative (use a memo), capability brief (use a memo), incident post-mortem (use a memo)

**Format-specific guidance:**
- The first sentence is for the ask or the conclusion. The second sentence supports it. Everything else is detail.
- If the email has more than three sections, it should be a memo.
- An email that ends with "please let me know your thoughts" usually has no actual ask. Replace with the specific action wanted.
- Attachments do not absolve the body. The body must stand on its own.

## 3. Leadership Status Update

**Purpose:** Recurring report (weekly, biweekly, monthly) that gives leadership a current view of a program, portfolio, or function. Updates compete for attention with every other update the executive reads.

**Conventions:**
- Headline at the top with a state indicator (on track, at risk, off track, achieved)
- Sections that are stable across updates so the reader knows where to look
- Deltas highlighted, not buried
- Specific asks, with named owners
- Length proportionate to substance, not to cadence (a quiet week is short)

**Soft length target:** 200 to 500 words. Updates that grow longer over time are usually accumulating sediment.

**Pattern compatibility:**
- Strongest: status update
- Workable: change announcement (when folded into an existing update), escalation (when one risk is critical)
- Awkward: decision request (use a dedicated memo), strategic narrative (does not fit the cadence)

**Format-specific guidance:**
- The headline is a sentence, not a label. "Q4 migration on track for January cutover" beats "Status: Green."
- Section structure should be predictable: same sections in the same order each cycle. The reader scans by location.
- "Deltas" should be the section the reader can read alone and still know what changed. If it cannot stand alone, the deltas are too dense.
- Asks belong in their own section, with names. "Mike to approve the contract by Friday" beats "approval needed."

## 4. Board or Steering Committee Briefing

**Purpose:** Formal communication to a governance body. Used when the audience holds fiduciary or strategic accountability and the communication will be part of the record.

**Conventions:**
- More formal register than other executive communications
- Options analysis where decisions are involved (board members expect to see what was considered)
- Risk treatment in standard categories (financial, regulatory, operational, reputational)
- Clear ownership of recommendations and decisions
- Materials package (pre-read, presentation, appendix) rather than a single document

**Soft length target:** Pre-read 1,500 to 3,000 words; supporting deck 8 to 15 slides; appendix as needed.

**Pattern compatibility:**
- Strongest: decision request, strategic narrative, incident or post-mortem
- Workable: capability or roadmap brief, reassurance
- Awkward: status update (use the standing report), change announcement (board does not announce, they ratify), technical explanation (only as appendix or context)

**Format-specific guidance:**
- Lead with the question being asked of the board. The board's job is to decide, advise, or ratify. Make that explicit at the top.
- Options analysis must be honest. Boards smell loaded options ("we recommend Option B, with Options A and C presented for completeness"). Present trade-offs as the operator sees them.
- Risk treatment must use the language the board uses. Match the company's risk taxonomy and the board's appetite framing.
- Avoid implementation detail. The board owns the "what" and "whether"; not the "how."

## 5. Presentation Deck

**Purpose:** Spoken or read presentation, used in-meeting or distributed for asynchronous review. The deck supports the narrative; it is not the narrative itself.

**Conventions:**
- Headline slides where the title carries the assertion: "Vendor A reduces total cost by 30% over three years" beats "Vendor comparison"
- One idea per slide
- Visuals that simplify a comparison, a relationship, or a trend; not visuals that decorate
- Body copy that supports the headline assertion (the audience reads the headline first)
- Appendix for detail that the spoken presentation does not need

**Soft length target:** 8 to 15 main slides for most executive contexts; 20 to 30 for board materials; appendix in addition.

**Pattern compatibility:**
- Strongest: strategic narrative, capability or roadmap brief, decision request (when the audience expects a deck), technical explanation
- Workable: status update (when the deck is the standing report), change announcement (when paired with a leader walk-through)
- Awkward: escalation (use email or call), reassurance (use a direct message), incident or post-mortem (use a memo, deck as supporting material)

**Format-specific guidance:**
- The headline of each slide is a sentence stating the assertion. If a reader could read only the headlines and still know the argument, the deck is well-structured.
- The body of the slide proves or develops the headline. If the body does not support the headline, one of them is wrong.
- Charts must be readable in five seconds. Mark the takeaway directly on the chart. Color is functional, not decorative.
- The first slide is the executive summary, not a title page. Restate the recommendation, the ask, or the headline finding.
- Do not include slides whose only purpose is structural (like a section divider that says "Background"). Use them sparingly or not at all.

## 6. Verbal Briefing or Talking Points

**Purpose:** Spoken communication, either delivered live by the author or used as a script for an executive to deliver. Used for prepared remarks, briefings to leadership, or speaker notes for a presentation.

**Conventions:**
- Spoken cadence, not written cadence. Shorter sentences, more signposting, more repetition than written prose allows.
- Anticipated objections and prepared responses
- Pivot phrases for handling difficult questions
- Numbers that can be said aloud and remembered (round, comparable, with context)
- Optional: explicit "if asked" or "if pressed" branches

**Soft length target:** Spoken content runs at roughly 150 words per minute. Match the target length to the time slot. Talking points alone are usually 200 to 600 words.

**Pattern compatibility:**
- Strongest: reassurance, escalation (verbal), decision request (when in-meeting), strategic narrative
- Workable: change announcement (when delivered by a leader), incident or post-mortem (when briefing the board verbally), capability or roadmap brief
- Awkward: status update (write it), technical explanation (write it, then talk over it)

**Format-specific guidance:**
- Use signposting that listeners need: "There are three things I want you to know." "First..." "Second..." "And third..."
- Avoid sentences that cannot be said in one breath. Read it aloud.
- Numbers in spoken form are rounded for memorability. "About a third" is more useful than "32.7 percent." Use the precise number once for credibility, then refer back to the rounded version.
- Prepared answers to the hard questions should be drafted in the same voice as the briefing.

## 7. Short-Form Leadership Message

**Purpose:** Quick, single-screen communication in Slack, Teams, or text. Used for time-sensitive updates, immediate asks, or brief acknowledgments. The format favors immediacy over polish.

**Conventions:**
- Fits on one screen without scrolling on a phone
- One ask or one piece of information per message
- No greeting boilerplate beyond what the channel expects
- Visible action in the first line
- Threading or follow-up replies for context, not a wall of text in the main message

**Soft length target:** 30 to 150 words. Longer belongs in email or a doc.

**Pattern compatibility:**
- Strongest: status update (when truly short), escalation (initial flag), change announcement (when supplementing a fuller comms), reassurance (when responding to a specific concern)
- Workable: decision request (when the decision is small or already pre-aligned)
- Awkward: strategic narrative (does not belong here), capability brief (does not belong here), incident post-mortem (use a memo or doc), technical explanation (use a doc)

**Format-specific guidance:**
- The first line is the action or the headline. No preamble.
- If the message exceeds the first screen, it should be a doc with a short link.
- Mentions should be deliberate, not pro forma. Mentioning everyone reduces signal.
- Tone is plainer and less hedged than email. The channel norms shape the register.

## Format Selection Guidance

When the format is not specified, choose based on objective and audience:

**Decision request:** memo for substantive decisions; email for narrow or pre-aligned decisions; board briefing if the decision goes to the board.

**Change announcement:** depends on who is being addressed. Memo or email for senior peers; broader internal comms (not covered by this skill) for the affected population.

**Status update:** the standing format used by that program. If no standing format, default to a written update.

**Escalation:** email for asynchronous; verbal briefing or short-form message for immediate; memo only if the situation needs documentation.

**Technical explanation:** memo if standalone; deck if it supports a discussion; talking points if delivered verbally.

**Strategic narrative:** deck for in-meeting use; memo for asynchronous distribution; often both.

**Incident or post-mortem:** memo as primary; deck or talking points only as supporting material.

**Reassurance:** match the channel of the concern. Email response to email; verbal response to verbal; short message to short message.

**Capability or roadmap brief:** deck for in-meeting use; memo for standalone reference; both for board contexts.

When in doubt, ask. The format affects how much rewriting changes the draft.
