# Disclosure Design and Fair Balance

Use this reference when assessing whether disclosures are complete, usable, and proportionate to claims.

## Fair Balance Tests

| Test | Reviewer question | Pass signal |
|---|---|---|
| Prominence | Are key limitations presented with meaningful prominence relative to benefits? | Risks and conditions are readable and not visually minimized |
| Proximity | Is disclosure close to the triggering claim in the same user flow? | Reader can access limitations without searching |
| Clarity | Is language understandable for the intended audience? | Plain language; avoids legal-only phrasing where possible |
| Completeness | Are material exclusions and conditions present? | No missing condition that would change interpretation |
| Channel fitness | Is disclosure usable in the channel format? | Timing and placement are practical for the medium |

## Channel Guidance

| Channel | Common failure mode | Preferred control |
|---|---|---|
| Social ad | Claim in image, disclosure buried off-platform | Include key limitation in-frame or immediate adjacent text |
| Landing page | CTA above fold, disclosure below long scroll | Surface critical qualifiers near headline and CTA |
| Email | Strong subject claim, no balancing qualifier in preheader/body opening | Add balancing qualifier in early body content |
| Video/script | Rapid benefit statements, unreadable end card disclaimer | Integrate spoken qualifiers and on-screen readable text |

## Readability Heuristics

- Avoid disclosures that rely only on tiny text.
- Avoid stacking multiple material caveats in a single dense sentence.
- Prefer direct, audience-readable wording over legal jargon where policy permits.
- Ensure disclosure references are specific to the triggering claim.
