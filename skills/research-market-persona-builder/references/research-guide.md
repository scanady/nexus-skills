# Research Guide: Building a Digital Twin Evidence Base

This guide provides a systematic protocol for researching a real person to build a high-fidelity digital twin persona. Research quality directly determines persona accuracy — surface-level research produces generic personas; deep research produces convincing advisors.

---

## Research Principles

1. **Evidence hierarchy matters.** Primary sources (the person's own words) outrank secondary sources (others' descriptions of them). Quote direct statements; summarize paraphrases.
2. **Behavior over claims.** What someone does under pressure is more revealing than what they say they believe. Document both and note divergence when it exists.
3. **Synthesis is interpretation.** When you draw a pattern from multiple sources, you are synthesizing — annotate it `[Synthesized]`, not `[Documented]`.
4. **Volume ≠ accuracy.** 10 high-quality, specific sources beat 50 thin hits. Prioritize depth over breadth.
5. **Recency weight.** For living people, prioritize sources from the last 5 years. Note when evidence is from an earlier era that may no longer reflect their current views.

---

## Research Protocol

### Phase 1: Biographical Foundation (Always First)

Establish who the person is before exploring how they think.

**Sources to check:**
- Wikipedia (biography sections; career timeline; notable works)
- Official website or personal site biography
- LinkedIn profile (career history, education, affiliations)
- Organizational bios (current and past employers, boards, academic appointments)

**Extract:**
- [ ] Full name and spelling variants (for search refinement)
- [ ] Birth year / generation
- [ ] Geographic context (where they grew up, where they work)
- [ ] Educational background (institutions, fields, degrees)
- [ ] Career arc with approximate years
- [ ] Major roles and the organizations associated with each
- [ ] Awards, recognitions, notable publications listed
- [ ] Professional affiliations, communities, associations

---

### Phase 2: Published Thinking (Primary Source Priority)

This is the richest evidence source. What people choose to publish reveals what they actually believe enough to defend publicly.

**For authors (books):**
- Read reviews AND summaries to extract core arguments if not reading the full text
- Note the central thesis of each major work
- Extract specific frameworks, models, or concepts they've named or developed
- Note vocabulary they use consistently — jargon they've coined or adopted

**For academics (papers):**
- Abstract + introduction + conclusion of key papers
- What problems do they study? What methods do they favor?
- What claims do they make that are contested in their field?

**For practitioners (articles, essays, newsletters):**
- Op-eds, thought leadership articles, published essays
- LinkedIn articles (for contemporary professionals)
- Substack or personal newsletters
- Notable published letters, memos, or white papers

**Search operators (adapt for any search engine):**
```
"[Full Name]" interview
"[Full Name]" keynote speech
"[Full Name]" advice
"[Full Name]" philosophy
"[Full Name]" book summary OR review
"[Full Name]" essay OR article
site:medium.com "[Full Name]"
```

**Extract:**
- [ ] Recurring themes across multiple works
- [ ] Specific frameworks or models they advocate
- [ ] Their vocabulary — terms they use repeatedly
- [ ] Their positions on contested questions in their domain
- [ ] Cases or examples they cite repeatedly (tells you what resonates with them)

---

### Phase 3: Interviews and Talks (Voice and Style)

Interviews reveal communication style, decision-making patterns, and unguarded opinions that polished writing doesn't.

**Sources by type:**

| Source Type | What It Reveals | Where to Find It |
|-------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Long-form podcast interviews | Thinking process, anecdotes, unpolished views | Spotify, Apple Podcasts, Lex Fridman, Tim Ferriss, How I Built This, etc. |
| Video keynotes | Communication style, emphasis, energy | YouTube, TED.com, conference sites |
| Print interviews | Polished views, notable quotes | Google News archive, trade publications |
| Q&A sessions | How they handle challenges, real opinions | Conference recordings, Reddit AMAs |
| Oral histories | Long retrospective reflection | Archives, oral history projects |

**Search strings:**
```
"[Full Name]" podcast interview [year]
"[Full Name]" ted talk
"[Full Name]" keynote [year]
"[Full Name]" ama OR "ask me anything"
"[Full Name]" profile [publication name]
```

**Extract from interviews:**
- [ ] Self-described decision-making approach ("I always ask myself...")
- [ ] Explicit mental models they name ("I use [framework] for...")
- [ ] Anecdotes about formative experiences
- [ ] Statements about what they look for in others they hire or mentor
- [ ] How they describe failure and what they learned
- [ ] Recurring phrases or verbal tics
- [ ] How they respond when challenged or asked hard questions

---

### Phase 4: Social Media and Short-Form (Real-Time Voice)

For living people, social media reveals current interests, real-time opinions, and casual communication style.

**Sources by platform:**

| Platform | What It Reveals |
|----------|----------------|
| X/Twitter | Real-time opinions, what they amplify, conversational style, debate behavior |
| LinkedIn | Professional positioning, endorsements, what they celebrate publicly |
| Substack/Newsletter | Sustained thinking on topics they care enough to write about regularly |
| Instagram/Threads | Interests outside professional domain, personal values signals |

**Key behaviors to observe on social media:**
- What topics do they post about most?
- How do they engage with criticism or pushback?
- Who do they amplify? (Reveals intellectual community)
- What do they celebrate publicly? (Reveals values)
- Posting frequency and texture: careful and polished vs. reactive and casual

**Note:** Social media can be misleading — performance and persuasion considerations shape public posting. Weight it lower than interviews and published works.

---

### Phase 5: Others on Them (Secondary Sources)

What credible others say about this person — especially people who've worked closely with them.

**Source types:**
- Profiles by journalists who've spent significant time with them
- Testimonials from former employees, students, or collaborators
- Academic citations and how their work is characterized by peers
- Critical assessments — what critics say reveals where they're contested

**Extract:**
- [ ] Descriptions of how they give feedback ("He always asks..."; "She insists...")
- [ ] Stories illustrating their character under pressure
- [ ] How people who've worked for/with them describe the experience
- [ ] Patterns in what critics consistently challenge

**Bias warning:** Consider the source's relationship to the subject. Former staff may be positive or resentful. Business press may focus on success narrative. Academic critics may emphasize oversimplification. Triangulate across multiple perspectives.

## Research by Subject Type

### Well-Known Public Figure (e.g. major author, prominent CEO, historical leader)

Full research protocol applies. Expect rich material across all five phases. Key risk: over-reliance on the hagiographic narrative. Seek out critical assessments and documented failures, not only success stories.

### Prominent Domain Expert (known publicly but not famous)

Phases 2–4 are most productive. Biographical info may be thin. Focus on published work and any available interviews. Ask the user for supplemental context.

### Professional Mentor or Advisor (known to user but limited public presence)

Public research will be sparse. Rely primarily on:
- User-supplied context (notes, observations, quotes)
- Any published work (articles, presentations, LinkedIn posts)
- User's direct experience of how this person advises

Frame user-supplied material as `[User-Supplied]`. Be explicit that the persona accuracy depends heavily on the quality of context provided.

### Historical Figure

Published works and documented accounts are primary sources. Biographies, letters, diaries (if available). Be explicit about the time-bounded nature of their positions — do not extrapolate their views into contemporary contexts without flagging it as inference.

---

## Research Quality Checklist

Before moving to synthesis, verify:

- [ ] At least 3 primary sources consulted (the person's own words)
- [ ] Communication style assessed from at least one spoken source (interview or talk)
- [ ] At least one critical or challenging source consulted (not only positive coverage)
- [ ] Source dates noted (to support confidence annotation about recency)
- [ ] User asked for supplemental context if public record is thin
- [ ] Quotes verified as attributed correctly (avoid misattributed quotes)

---

## Common Research Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Confusing a popular paraphrase with a direct quote | Use quotation marks only for verbatim text; use "described as" for paraphrases |
| Taking press releases or corporate bios at face value | Triangulate with third-party sources and interviews |
| Treating Wikipedia as a primary source | Use as a starting point; follow citations to primary sources |
| Over-indexing on recent social media vs. sustained body of work | Weight longer-form published work more heavily for intellectual content |
| Assuming consistency over time for living people | Note the era of each source; flag when positions may have evolved |
| Conflating their domain views with personal values | Distinguish professional positions from personal ethics — both matter but are separate |
