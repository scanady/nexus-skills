# Failure Audit

Use this reference when reviewing, critiquing, or revising content for argument quality, evidence discipline, analogy strength, and tone.

## Severity Guide

| Severity | Meaning |
|---|---|
| HIGH | The flaw can make the conclusion false, misleading, or materially weaker |
| MED | The flaw reduces trust, clarity, or usefulness but can be repaired locally |
| LOW | The flaw is a polish issue, tone issue, or caveat that should be tightened |

## 1. Narrative Fallacy

**Flaw:** The story is so compelling that the writer bends the data to fit it.

**How to spot it:** Remove the anecdote, character profile, origin story, or dramatic scene. Does the remaining evidence still support the conclusion? If the argument collapses without its emotional coat of paint, the story is carrying weight the data cannot bear.

**Fix:** Rebuild the claim around the strongest evidence first, then reintroduce the story only where it illustrates the mechanism. Add caveats where the story is atypical.

## 2. Over-Generalization Trap

**Flaw:** A narrow study, small sample, lab condition, or specific domain is used to explain a broad human or societal pattern.

**How to spot it:** Check sample size, context, population, incentive structure, time period, and replication. Weak phrasing says, "This proves people always behave this way." Strong phrasing says, "This suggests a pattern under these conditions."

**Fix:** Narrow the claim. State the conditions. Name what the evidence does and does not justify.

## 3. Just-So Storytelling

**Flaw:** The writer looks at an outcome that already happened, selects only data points that lead to it, and presents the result as a predictable rule.

**How to spot it:** Ask whether the same logic could explain the opposite outcome. If success is attributed to being scrappy, check whether many failed actors were also scrappy. Look for survivorship bias and missing comparison groups.

**Fix:** Add base rates, counterexamples, failed cases, or alternative explanations. Replace inevitability with a testable mechanism.

## 4. False Equivalency in Analogies

**Flaw:** An analogy feels clever but misrepresents how the real mechanism works.

**How to spot it:** Break the analogy into mapped parts. Does it preserve feedback loops, decentralized behavior, incentives, panic, compounding, bottlenecks, scarcity, uncertainty, and scale? If not, it may make the reader feel smarter while understanding less.

**Fix:** State the analogy's limit directly, choose a better analogy, or replace the analogy with a mechanism explanation.

## 5. Hidden Ideology Masked as Neutrality

**Flaw:** The writer claims to be "just looking at the numbers" while selecting evidence to support a political, economic, cultural, or philosophical agenda.

**How to spot it:** Look at what is missing. Are the strongest counterarguments acknowledged? Are mainstream explanations treated seriously? Are alternative hypotheses described as stupid instead of plausible but weaker?

**Fix:** Add the strongest competing explanation, distinguish values from facts, and state the writer's assumptions. If the piece has a point of view, own it honestly.

## 6. Intellectual Smugness

**Flaw:** The writer prizes the reversal more than the truth and uses contrarianism as performance.

**How to spot it:** Watch for mocking language, contempt for outsiders, excessive "everyone is wrong" framing, or delight in proving conventional wisdom foolish. Sometimes conventional wisdom is correct because it is a compressed form of hard-won experience.

**Fix:** Replace contempt with curiosity. Keep the reversal only where the evidence supports it. Acknowledge where the conventional view remains useful.

## Hype and Panic Check

Ask:

- Is the piece amplifying fear, awe, or urgency beyond the evidence?
- Does it treat a trend as destiny?
- Does it confuse a demo, anecdote, or early adoption curve with a settled future?
- Does it moralize before it explains?

Fix by adding timelines, adoption constraints, second-order effects, and concrete uncertainty.

## Review Checklist

Use these questions before final delivery:

- What is the central claim, in one plain sentence?
- What is the strongest evidence for it?
- What evidence would weaken it?
- Where does the sample, anecdote, or analogy stop applying?
- Does the argument acknowledge the best counterargument?
- Could the same explanation fit the opposite outcome?
- What does the reader know how to do after reading?
- What sentence sounds clever but may be doing no real work?

## Recommended Finding Format

Use this format in Review mode:

```
- [HIGH|MED|LOW] Passage: flaw name - why it weakens the piece - specific fix.
```

Keep findings concrete. Quote only the smallest useful phrase or passage. Prefer fixes that preserve the writer's best idea while tightening the evidence and claim boundary.
