# Worked Examples

Use this reference when calibrating the skill's output, teaching the pattern, or resolving uncertainty about how to structure a draft, critique, or outline.

## Example 1: Earned Aha Pivot

Weak version:

> Everyone thinks remote work is about location, but the real issue is trust.

Why it is weak: The reversal is plausible, but it is too broad and unsupported. It sounds like insight before it earns insight.

Stronger version:

> Remote work looks like a debate about location because location is the part managers can see. But the harder variable is response time: who gets an answer quickly, who waits, and which decisions quietly move to private channels. In that sense, the office did not create trust by putting people in the same room. It hid trust failures by making access feel automatic.

Why it works: It starts from the visible assumption, introduces a specific hidden mechanism, narrows the reversal, and gives the reader a concrete thing to inspect.

## Example 2: False Analogy Review Finding

Passage:

> A large language model is like a very smart intern. Give it clear instructions and it will do the work.

Review finding:

- [MED] "like a very smart intern": false analogy - the comparison helps explain delegation, but it hides the mechanism that matters most: the model has no durable situational memory, no independent accountability, and no stable understanding of truth. Replace with an analogy that preserves the need for verification, or state the analogy's limit directly.

Possible revision:

> A language model is less like an intern and more like a fast simulator of plausible work. Clear instructions improve the simulation. They do not remove the need to check whether the answer is true, current, or appropriate for the situation.

## Example 3: Shape Mode Outline

Topic: Why small AI experiments often teach more than abstract AI strategy.

Thesis: AI strategy gets clearer when teams run small, real experiments because the hard questions are usually behavioral, not technical.

Reader turn: The useful question is not "What can AI do?" but "Where does our work break when a capable but unreliable assistant enters the loop?"

Evidence plan:

- Compare a generic AI policy discussion with one team running the same task three ways: human-only, AI-assisted, and AI-first with review.
- Track where time moves: drafting, checking, coordination, rework.
- Note where confidence rises faster than quality.

Scale anchor: Treat each workflow like a kitchen timer test: not a grand theory of cooking, just a way to see where the minutes actually go.

Human story or experiment: A manager asks three team members to summarize the same customer call with and without AI, then compares what each version misses.

Utility payoff: Readers can run a one-hour workflow test before buying tools, rewriting org charts, or issuing sweeping rules.

Risks to avoid:

- Do not claim the small test proves all AI impact.
- Do not imply experiments replace security, legal, or quality controls.
- Do not treat speed as value unless the downstream work still improves.

Suggested outline:

1. Open with a team debating AI strategy in abstract terms.
2. Pivot to the small experiment that exposes the real bottleneck.
3. Explain what the experiment measures and what it cannot measure.
4. Show the surprising result: checking, coordination, or confidence becomes the constraint.
5. Translate the result into a practical test readers can run.
