# Craft System

Use this reference when drafting, shaping, or deeply revising critical explanatory content.

## Core Mental Frameworks

### Laboratory Lens

Treat the world as a living experiment. Everyday behavior, institutional incentives, macro-systems, market shifts, and emerging technology are not props for opinion. They are observations to test.

Write from curiosity before judgment:

- What is happening?
- What changed?
- Who has an incentive to behave this way?
- What would we expect if the common explanation were true?
- What would we expect if the opposite explanation were true?
- What can the reader test in their own work or life?

### First-Principles Thinking

Strip away the vocabulary that makes a topic feel owned by experts. Rebuild it from fundamentals:

- Actors: who is doing something?
- Incentives: what do they gain, avoid, signal, protect, or risk?
- Constraints: what limits their choices?
- Mechanism: how does cause become effect?
- Scale: how big, small, fast, slow, rare, or common is it?
- Feedback: what changes after the first response?
- Tradeoff: what gets better, and what gets worse?

Replace gatekeeping language with universal language. Do not make the topic smaller. Make the door wider.

## Signature Moves

### The "Aha!" Pivot

Use inversion only when it is earned. A strong pivot has five parts:

1. Conventional assumption: what most people think.
2. Hidden observation: the ignored fact, incentive, mechanism, or denominator.
3. Reversal: how the observation changes the interpretation.
4. Consequence: what this means for decisions, work, behavior, or risk.
5. Boundary: where the reversal stops being true.

Weak pivot: "Everyone thinks X, but they are wrong."

Strong pivot: "X looks true from the visible data. But when you include Y, the mechanism changes. X is still true in this narrow case, but Z is the better rule for the decision most people face."

### Scale Analogies

Use scale analogies to make invisible concepts tangible. Anchor numbers and mechanisms to familiar experience:

- Time: seconds, workdays, lifetimes, generations.
- Space: desks, rooms, streets, cities, continents.
- Money: groceries, rent, salaries, budgets, household tradeoffs.
- Attention: browser tabs, meetings, notifications, choices per day.
- Risk: repeated bets, insurance, queues, bottlenecks, failure rates.

Always test the analogy:

- What part maps cleanly?
- What part does not map?
- Does the analogy preserve feedback loops?
- Does it preserve scale and compounding?
- Does it preserve human behavior under stress?

If the analogy only makes the sentence clever, cut it.

### Character-Driven Data

Data lands harder when attached to a real choice, person, experiment, researcher, failure, or small observable scene. Use human stories to reveal mechanisms, not to smuggle in conclusions.

Good uses:

- A researcher whose odd method exposes a hidden variable.
- A worker whose daily choice reveals a macro incentive.
- A failed company that shows what the success story hides.
- A personal experiment that makes a technical frontier concrete.
- A historical episode that rhymes with the current system without pretending history repeats exactly.

Guardrail: the data must still support the conclusion if the colorful story is removed.

### Immediate Utility

The reader should leave with a usable lens, not just a satisfying explanation. Convert insight into action:

- A question to ask before buying, building, investing, hiring, voting, adopting, or quitting.
- A simple test the reader can run.
- A sign that a system is changing.
- A boundary condition that prevents misuse.
- A decision rule with caveats.

## Signature Style Modes

### Pragmatic Guide

Feels like a brilliant peer leaning over the reader's shoulder after running the numbers or trying the tool. Use for technology, work, AI, productivity, experiments, career decisions, and practical systems.

Voice signals:

- First person is allowed when grounded in a real test.
- Concrete before abstract.
- "Here is what happened" beats "this demonstrates."
- Useful imperfection beats theoretical completeness.

### Hidden History

Connects dots across time to explain a confusing present. Use for markets, institutions, incentives, social norms, technologies, infrastructure, and policy.

Voice signals:

- Start with a scene, artifact, rule, failure, or forgotten choice.
- Show how a local decision scaled into a modern pattern.
- Avoid inevitability. Include missed alternatives, failed paths, and accidents.
- Bring the history back to a current decision or lens.

## Prose Texture

Aim for prose that is conversational, precise, energetic, and skeptical in the clean sense: willing to be surprised, unwilling to be sold.

Prefer:

- Active verbs.
- Specific nouns.
- Short explanations of mechanism.
- Concrete stakes.
- Controlled wit.
- Honest uncertainty.
- Rhythm variation.

Avoid:

- Academic passive voice.
- Expert gatekeeping.
- Trend panic.
- Breathless adjectives.
- Moralizing without analysis.
- Contrarian performance.
- Certainty theater.
