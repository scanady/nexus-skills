# Reframing Patterns

Named moves for helping a person see a feasible path to the same intent. Each pattern preserves the original goal and changes the route. Use them as lenses to offer, not prescriptions to impose. The user decides which one fits.

The throughline: the person came in wanting to deliver something. None of these abandon that. They change what the thing is, how it works, or what counts as done, so the intent survives.

## Narrow the target

The goal tries to do everything; make it do one thing well.

- Generalist becomes specialist. "Instead of an analyst for every question, what if it owned one high-value category really well?"
- Broad audience becomes a specific user with a specific job.
- A platform becomes a single workflow that earns the right to expand later.

When to use: the person is overwhelmed by scope or the system underperforms because it is spread thin.

## Change the role, not the capability

Keep what the thing does, shift how it participates.

- Decider becomes advisor. "What if it does not make the call, it surfaces the data, the options, and the questions worth asking next?" This is often the unlock for anything in a regulated or high-stakes setting: it sidesteps the accuracy-and-liability wall while still delivering value.
- Autonomous becomes assistive, with a human in the loop on the risky step.
- Answer-giver becomes question-raiser.

When to use: the constraint is about trust, risk, accuracy, or accountability rather than raw capability.

## Redefine done

The bar for success is set higher than the need requires.

- Perfect becomes good enough to act on.
- Comprehensive becomes the twenty percent that drives eighty percent of the value.
- Fully automated becomes mostly automated with a clean handoff for the rest.

When to use: the person is chasing a standard that nothing, including a human, actually meets.

## Add a guardrail instead of a capability

Rather than making the system smarter, make a known weakness safe.

- Pair an uncertain output with a confidence signal or a "verify this" flag.
- Constrain the input space so the system only operates where it is reliable.
- Put a review gate on the one step where a mistake is costly.

When to use: the worry is safety or accuracy and the fix is containment, not capability.

## Reframe the problem behind the problem

Sometimes the stated problem is a symptom. Ask what the person is really trying to achieve.

- "The team wants the agent to answer everything. But what do they actually need? Faster decisions? Then the win might be surfacing the right three questions, not answering all of them."
- Trace from the feature back to the underlying need, then re-solve for the need.

When to use: effort is going into a solution that, even if it worked, might not deliver the real outcome.

## Sequence it

The whole thing is not feasible now; a piece of it is.

- Ship the part that works, learn, expand. Phase the ambition rather than dropping it.
- Prove the risky assumption cheaply before committing to the full build.

When to use: the goal is sound but the all-at-once version is what is blocked.

## Using these in conversation

Offer one lens at a time and let it breathe. Ask a question from inside the new frame rather than announcing the frame itself. Check whether it resonates before offering another. The reframe only works if the person adopts it as their own; one you talked them into will not survive contact with their team.
