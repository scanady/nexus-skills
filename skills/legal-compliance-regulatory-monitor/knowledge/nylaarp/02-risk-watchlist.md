# AARP Life Insurance Program — Compliance Risk Watchlist

**Program:** AARP Life Insurance Program (NYL/AARP)  
**Last Updated:** 2025  
**Purpose:** Identifies the top compliance risks specific to this program's marketing materials and distribution channels. Use this watchlist in every creative review and prior to campaign launch.

---

## Risk Severity Key

| Level | Meaning |
|---|---|
| CRITICAL | Regulatory violation or enforcement exposure; material must not run without correction |
| HIGH | Significant non-compliance risk; requires mandatory correction before approval |
| MEDIUM | Potential violation; requires legal/compliance judgment call before approval |
| LOW | Best practice gap; recommend correction but may be approved with documented rationale |

---

## RISK 1 — Graded Benefit Misrepresentation (Guaranteed Acceptance)

**Severity:** CRITICAL  
**Product:** Guaranteed Acceptance Whole Life  
**Standards:** NAIC Model Advertising Reg IND-06; FTC FED-01; NY ST-NY-01

### The Risk
Guaranteed Acceptance products lead with "guaranteed" and "no health questions required" — powerful claims for an audience with health concerns. The mandatory 2-year waiting period (graded benefit) means beneficiaries receive only 110% of premiums paid — not the full death benefit — if the insured dies of natural causes within the first 2 policy years. If this limitation is buried, minimized, or absent, the advertising creates a materially false impression.

### What Triggers This Risk
- Headline or primary call-to-action uses "guaranteed acceptance" or "no health questions" without adjacent graded benefit disclosure
- Waiting period disclosure appears only in fine print, asterisk, or below the fold
- Visual design draws eye to "guaranteed" and away from limitations
- Digital ads (banner, social) that lack sufficient space for disclosure and omit waiting period entirely
- Testimonials or scenarios that imply full benefit was received without mentioning the waiting period

### Required Control
> **Mandatory disclosure in all Guaranteed Acceptance materials:**  
> *"This policy has a waiting period. If the insured dies of natural causes in the first [2] years, the benefit paid to your beneficiaries will be [110%] of the premiums you have paid, not the full death benefit."*  
> (Adapt to current certificate language; placement must be prominent — not footnoted below visual)

## RISK 2 — Term Life Premium Escalation Not Disclosed

**Severity:** CRITICAL  
**Product:** Level Benefit Term Life  
**Standards:** NAIC IND-05; FTC FED-01, FED-02; NY ST-NY-02

### The Risk
Term life premiums increase every time the insured enters a new 5-year age band. A consumer who enters at age 50 paying $91/month will pay $144 at 55–59, $218 at 60–64, and so on. If advertising promotes a low initial premium without disclosing this escalation structure, it creates a materially false impression of cost.

### What Triggers This Risk
- Rate tables or "starting at" rates shown without note that rates increase with age
- Direct mail or landing pages showing only the applicant's current age rate without adjacent disclosure of future increases
- Headline claims like "low monthly rates" without rate escalation disclosure
- Long-form copy that mentions premium structure in a way that could be misread as level

### Required Control
> **Mandatory disclosure in all Term Life rate advertising:**  
> *"Rates increase when you enter each new 5-year age band."*  
> Or equivalent — must appear in proximity to any quoted or implied rate.

## RISK 3 — Coverage Amount Ceiling Not Communicated

**Severity:** HIGH  
**Products:** All products  
**Standards:** NAIC IND-01; FTC FED-01, FED-02

### The Risk
Standard online limits are $150,000 (Term), $100,000 (Whole Life), and $30,000 (Guaranteed Acceptance). If materials emphasize "get the coverage you need" or imply unlimited or high coverage without stating actual limits, they create a false impression about coverage capacity.

### What Triggers This Risk
- Phrases like "coverage for whatever you leave behind" without ceiling statement
- Visuals of large estates, expensive final expenses, etc. without context on coverage limits
- Benefit calculators or needs estimators that produce outputs higher than available coverage without a caveat

### Required Control
State actual coverage maximum or include: *"Coverage amounts up to $[X] available online. Higher amounts may be available by calling [number]."*

## RISK 4 — "Guaranteed" Language Without Limitation Disclosure

**Severity:** CRITICAL  
**Products:** Guaranteed Acceptance Whole Life  
**Standards:** NAIC IND-01, IND-06; FTC FED-01, FED-02

### The Risk
The word "guaranteed" appears in the product name and is a central marketing message. Regulatory guidance consistently treats stand-alone "guaranteed" claims in insurance advertising as inherently misleading unless limitations are disclosed with equal prominence. This risk extends beyond just the waiting period — it includes guaranteed acceptance claims without disclosing that "natural causes" deaths in year 1–2 do not receive the full benefit.

### What Triggers This Risk
- "Guaranteed acceptance" in headline without graded benefit in same visual field
- "Your acceptance is guaranteed" in body copy without adjacent limitation
- Social media posts or display ads that use "guaranteed" as the only copy and link to a landing page with buried disclosures

### Required Control
Treat "guaranteed" as a trigger word requiring adjacent limitation disclosure in all placements, including space-constrained digital formats.

## RISK 5 — AARP Endorsement / "Best for Seniors" Claims

**Severity:** HIGH  
**Products:** All products  
**Standards:** FTC FED-03; CO-05; Class action precedent from Krukas v. AARP

### The Risk
In *Krukas v. AARP* (D.D.C. 2018), plaintiffs alleged AARP misrepresented its branded insurance policies as "best for seniors" without disclosing that AARP receives royalty compensation from New York Life for licensing its brand. While the case was ultimately dismissed, the litigation pattern reveals regulatory and legal exposure when AARP's financial interest in the product is not disclosed.

The FTC's endorsement guidelines require disclosure of material connections between an endorser and advertiser. AARP's royalty interest is a material connection.

### What Triggers This Risk
- "AARP-endorsed," "AARP-recommended," "AARP's preferred" without disclosure of licensing relationship
- Implication that AARP independently evaluated and selected NYL as the best insurer for seniors
- AARP branding used in a way that implies AARP is acting as an objective consumer advocate rather than a licensing partner

### Required Control
All materials should accurately characterize the relationship. Recommended disclosure context:  
*"The AARP Life Insurance Program is issued by New York Life Insurance Company. New York Life pays royalties to AARP for use of AARP's intellectual property. These royalties are used for the general purposes of AARP."*  
(Adapt to current disclosure language approved by AARP legal)

## RISK 6 — Fear, Urgency, and Decline Framing

**Severity:** HIGH  
**Products:** All products  
**Channels:** Direct mail, call center, digital  
**Standards:** FTC FED-01; AARP anti-ageism brand standards; CA ST-CA-01; FL ST-FL-01; state AG senior marketing guidance

### The Risk
AARP has formal anti-ageism advertising commitments and publicly opposed marketing that portrays people 50+ as confused, incapacitated, frightened, or in decline. Marketing that uses fear of death, health decline, or financial ruin as primary motivators — without providing balanced, empowering framing — violates both AARP brand standards and FTC senior marketing guidance. Some states (CA, FL) have elder financial protection statutes that treat fear-based marketing as a form of financial exploitation.

### What Triggers This Risk
- Imagery of frail elders, hospital settings, grief, or family financial ruin as primary visual
- Headlines like "Don't leave your family with nothing" or "What happens to your family when you're gone?"
- Countdown clocks or limited-time enrollment urgency in direct mail or digital ads without a genuine enrollment window
- "Your age could disqualify you soon" or similar deadline pressure
- Call center scripts that reference health vulnerability as a primary reason to buy now

### Required Control
- Imagery must show 50+ adults as active, capable, and engaged (per AARP brand commitments)
- Urgency claims must be based on genuine, verifiable enrollment windows — not manufactured scarcity
- Reframe: benefits-forward, empowerment-oriented language
- Escalate any script or concept using health fear or family distress as primary driver to legal review

## RISK 7 — AARP Membership Requirement Not Disclosed

**Severity:** HIGH  
**Products:** All products  
**Standards:** NAIC IND-01; FTC FED-01, FED-02

### The Risk
AARP membership is required to purchase coverage. If advertising presents coverage as broadly available to adults 50+ without disclosing the membership requirement, it creates a false impression and generates applicant confusion, downstream customer service issues, and potential regulatory complaints.

### What Triggers This Risk
- "Available to adults age 50 and older" without mentioning AARP membership requirement
- Landing page or quote flow that collects personal information before surfacing membership requirement
- Teaser advertising that drives to nylaarp.com without mentioning AARP membership

### Required Control
State AARP membership requirement in all advertising, not only at application stage.  
*"Available to AARP members age 50 and older."*

## RISK 8 — Living Benefits Misframing (Accelerated Death Benefit)

**Severity:** HIGH  
**Product:** Whole Life with Living Benefits  
**Standards:** NAIC IND-01; FTC FED-01, FED-02; CO-07

### The Risk
The living benefits feature allows access to up to 50% of the face amount while alive — but only upon diagnosis of a qualifying illness. It is not a savings account, cash value access, or general-purpose benefit. Framing it as broadly accessible cash or a financial planning tool without disclosing the qualifying illness requirement is misleading.

Additionally, this product is not available in all states. Using it in a national campaign without state availability disclosure risks advertising a product that is not available to a portion of the audience.

### What Triggers This Risk
- "Access your benefit while you're still alive" without "upon qualifying illness" modifier
- Framing as "living benefit" (brand name) without explaining the triggering condition
- National campaign materials for this product without state availability disclaimer
- Comparison to cash value life insurance without disclosing the qualifying illness restriction

### Required Control
Always pair "living benefit" with "if you are diagnosed with a qualifying illness" in the same sentence or adjacent disclosure.  
State availability disclaimer required in any national placement.

## RISK 9 — State Availability Omissions

**Severity:** CRITICAL for NJ; HIGH for NY age bands  
**Products:** Guaranteed Acceptance (NJ, NY); Living Benefits (multi-state)  
**Standards:** ST-NJ-01, ST-NJ-02; ST-NY-03; CO-07

### The Risk
Guaranteed Acceptance Whole Life is not available in New Jersey. Guaranteed Acceptance has different age eligibility in New York (50–75 vs. 50–85 in most states). Materials that fail to reflect these restrictions expose NYL and AARP to regulatory complaints and consumer harm.

### What Triggers This Risk
- National direct mail piece for Guaranteed Acceptance mailed to NJ residents
- Online landing page for Guaranteed Acceptance without NJ exclusion
- Age band stated as "ages 50 to 85" without noting NY's 50–75 restriction
- Quote tool that allows NJ or age-75+ NY consumers to advance through the application for Guaranteed Acceptance

### Required Control
- All Guaranteed Acceptance materials must include: *"Not available in New Jersey."*
- NY-targeted materials must use NY-specific age bands (50–75)
- Digital application must block ineligible applicants at intake, not at submission

## RISK 10 — Replacement / Inducement to Lapse Existing Coverage

**Severity:** HIGH  
**Products:** All products  
**Standards:** NAIC IND-11; CA ST-CA-02; state replacement regulations

### The Risk
Many AARP members who receive this marketing already have life insurance coverage. If advertising implies that AARP/NYL coverage is superior to their current coverage in a way that would induce lapsing, without proper replacement notice disclosures, it violates NAIC replacement regulations and state-specific rules.

### What Triggers This Risk
- "Switch today" or "replace your old policy" framing
- "Don't rely on coverage from another company" without providing replacement disclosure
- Comparison charts that disparage existing coverage types without factual basis
- Call center scripts that ask applicants to describe their current coverage and then use that information to disparage the existing insurer

### Required Control
- Never use language that explicitly induces replacement
- If replacement is discussed, provide required state replacement notice at point of sale
- Call center scripts must not request existing policy details for competitive comparison purposes
