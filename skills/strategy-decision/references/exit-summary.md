# Exit Summary

How to recognize readiness and close well. The exit is what turns a good conversation into a usable outcome. Done right, the person walks away with something tangible they can act on and share.

## Recognizing genuine readiness

Exit when the person has real conviction, not when they have run out of energy. The single sharpest test: have they crossed from "this won't work" to "here's how I make it work"? Ask them to restate the path in their own words and name the first move. A route they can describe means they are over the hump. "I feel clearer" with no describable route means they are not.

Signs of real readiness:

- They can state, in their own words, the path forward and the first concrete move.
- They sound like the problem got smaller or clearer, not like they gave up.
- They answer the confidence check ("do you feel the risk is mitigated?", "do you feel you have a plan?") with a genuine yes, not a tired one.

Watch for the quit-instinct disguised as a landing. "Maybe this just isn't doable," "I'll come back to it later," or a sudden shrug is someone stepping away from the pit, not climbing out of it. Name it and hold the line: "That sounds more like stepping away than a plan. Before you do, let's find the version that works." Do not let that count as an exit.

Signs they are NOT ready, even if they say they are:

- They agree quickly but cannot restate the plan themselves.
- The confidence is in your analysis, not in their own sense of the path.
- They have a reframe but no next move.
- They went quiet and the "I think I am good" reads as fatigue.

If they are not ready, do not exit. Surface it kindly: "Before we wrap, I want to make sure this feels solid to you and not just settled. Can you walk me through what you would do Monday morning?"

## Two honest exit shapes

1. **They have what they need and just had not seen it.** The reframe or the existing pieces add up to a path; help them see it and name it back.
2. **They genuinely do not have what they need yet.** Sometimes the honest landing is "the real next step is to go find out X" or "the constraint is real and the move is to escalate or rescope with your team." A clear next step into uncertainty is still a valid, momentum-preserving exit. Do not manufacture false confidence to close.

## The summary itself

Keep it bulleted and tight. It is an artifact to carry forward and share with a team, not a recap of the conversation. Use the structure in the main SKILL.md. Notes on each part:

- **Original goal:** state it as they framed it coming in, so the throughline is visible.
- **Reframed as:** if the framing shifted, capture the new shape clearly. If it did NOT shift, say so plainly and note why the original holds; not every problem needs reframing, and pretending otherwise is dishonest.
- **Success looks like:** the concrete definition you landed on, in their terms.
- **Key constraints, and what we did about them:** pair each real blocker with its mitigation or decision. This is the part a skeptical teammate will scrutinize, so make it specific.
- **The path over the hump:** the route from "won't work" to "here's how," written as something they could actually walk. This is the line that proves they crossed, not just felt better.
- **Your next steps:** specific and owned. "Draft the narrowed scope and run it past the team Thursday" beats "think about scope."

Close with their stated confidence level in their own words and one genuine line of encouragement toward the move. Earned, not effusive.

## After the exit

Stop. Do not reopen the analysis or introduce a fresh challenge once the summary is delivered. The job was to get them moving, and they are moving. If they come back with a new wrinkle, that is a new pass, not a continuation of the close.
