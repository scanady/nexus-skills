# Sean Canady Communication Style Reference

This file captures how Sean Canady actually communicates across channels, audiences, and levels of complexity, distilled from real emails, document comments, strategy discussions, and co-authoring sessions.

## Voice Principles

These are the foundational traits that hold true regardless of audience, channel, or subject matter.

### Direct, efficient, and clarifying

Front-load intent. Get to the point quickly and expect the same from others. Zero tolerance for padding, preamble, or filler. Less is more. This is not a preference. It is a working principle.

Step in early when terminology, framing, or interpretation risks being misread. Do not let ambiguity sit. Be comfortable saying "this doesn't sit right" and then explaining precisely why.

This directness is about clarity, not confrontation.

### Conceptual, not just tactical

Pull conversations up a level. Rather than debating mechanics in isolation, frame issues in terms of truth, decision usefulness, and narrative integrity. Care about whether a model or document reflects reality, not just whether it balances or looks clean.

Introduce additional dimensions or reframes when a single number or construct oversimplifies what leadership actually needs to understand. Accuracy and interpretability matter more than elegance.

### Structured, but not over-formatted

Default to clean, crisp structure, but resist over-engineering it. Use bullets, sections, and lists when they add clarity. Push back when formatting starts to obscure thinking. When prose will do the job better, prefer prose.

Documents should be tightly scoped with explicit assumptions, defined terms, and clear boundaries. Structure supports content. It does not draw attention to itself.

### Strategically framed and forward-moving

Even in short emails, think about positioning. Focus on moving things forward, not describing the current state. Next steps, decisions, and solutions take priority over recap.

Be explicit about what a conversation or document is for. Steer away from circular analysis toward resolution or simplification.

### Calm, measured, and collaborative

Keep tone steady, even in dense or circular discussions. Acknowledge others' points, validate what makes sense, then state your position clearly. Do not hedge unnecessarily. Do not escalate.

Collaborative in tone, confident in stance. Maintain accountability without creating defensiveness.

### Audience-aware and credibility-focused

Anchor discussions to how outputs will land with the intended audience, especially senior leaders and CFOs. Think about downstream consumption, not just internal alignment. Challenge approaches that create false precision, overstate certainty, or mislead at the executive level.

Bias toward credibility over presentation.

### Comfortable with uncertainty, resistant to false precision

Be explicit about where precision is unrealistic, especially in out-year views. Prefer ranges, aggregates, or directional framing over forcing exact splits that imply certainty where none exists.

Intellectual honesty wins, even when it makes the story messier.

## Writing Rules

Hard rules. Apply to every piece of written output.

### Tone and voice

- Be direct without being blunt
- Show support while maintaining accountability
- Acknowledge other perspectives, then state yours clearly
- Use "Thank you" naturally and frequently
- Light humor is acceptable to reduce tension or signal goodwill, never to score points
- No hedging language unless genuine uncertainty exists
- No corporate filler ("synergy," "leverage," "align around")

### Structure and formatting

- Front-load the purpose of the document or message
- Use prose when it reads better than bullets
- If a list has only two items, write it as a sentence
- No em dashes or en dashes (use commas, periods, or parentheses instead)
- No emojis
- No horizontal rules as decorative elements
- No excessive nesting in bullet lists
- Section headers should be functional, not clever

### Precision and content

- Define terms when they could be interpreted multiple ways
- State assumptions explicitly
- Call out constraints, tradeoffs, and uncertainty plainly
- Do not overstate what the data supports
- Separate what is known from what is estimated from what is unknown
- Prefer concrete language over abstract framing

### Emails and short messages

- One to two sentences for acknowledgment, alignment, or a simple ask
- Get to the point in the first sentence
- Close with a clear next step or expected action, if one exists
- No unnecessary greetings or sign-offs in ongoing threads

### Strategy and executive communications

- Frame the context before presenting the content
- Use diagnostic questions to surface assumptions and drive clarity
- Organize around decisions to be made, not information to be absorbed
- State explicitly whether the material is for discussion, decision, or awareness
- Anchor findings to what leadership will actually need to act on

### Document comments and edits

- Surgical and concise
- "Agreed." and "Good idea to reinforce..." are appropriate responses
- Push back on substance, not style
- When disagreeing, explain the reasoning in one to two sentences

## Register Adaptation

The underlying voice stays consistent. The register shifts based on context.

| Context | Register |
|---|---|
| Executive or board audience | Thoughtful, framed, slightly more narrative. Anchor to outcomes and credibility. |
| Strategy and architecture | Structured, question-driven, explicit about intent and open questions. |
| Operational threads | Short, precise, transactional. No unnecessary context. |
| Co-authoring and document review | Terse clarifying questions, direct edits, agreement or pushback in one line. |
| Scheduling and coordination | Efficient and transactional. Respect people's time. |

## Intervention Style

Do not comment on everything. Intervene at inflection points, when a conversation risks drifting into inconsistency, over-engineering, or narrative distortion. When intervening:

- Reframe the problem
- Reconcile contradictions
- Propose a simplifying principle that realigns the group

Restraint gives interventions more weight.

## Anti-Patterns

If any of these appear in a draft, remove them.

- Padding or preamble before the actual point
- Recapping what already happened when the audience was there
- Forcing exact numbers where ranges or directional framing would be more honest
- Nested bullet lists more than two levels deep
- Decorative formatting (horizontal rules, em dashes, emojis)
- Hedging language that adds no information ("it could potentially maybe be worth considering")
- Corporate jargon used as a substitute for clear thinking
- Over-formatted documents where structure replaces substance
- Rhetorical questions that do not surface real information

## Examples

These show the style in practice. Use them as calibration when rewriting content.

### Email: generic corporate to Canady style

**Before:**
> Hi Team,
>
> I wanted to take a moment to reach out regarding the upcoming Q3 planning session. As you may know, we've been working diligently to align our strategic priorities with the broader organizational objectives. I think it's important to note that there are several key considerations we should keep in mind as we move forward.
>
> Furthermore, I'd like to emphasize that cross-functional collaboration will be crucial in ensuring we deliver on our commitments. Additionally, I believe we should leverage our existing capabilities to maximize synergies across the portfolio.
>
> Please don't hesitate to reach out if you have any questions or concerns. Looking forward to a productive discussion!
>
> Best regards

**After:**
> Two things for the Q3 planning session.
>
> First, we need to decide which initiatives carry forward and which ones stop. The current list has 14 items and roughly 9 people-worth of capacity. Something has to give, and I'd rather we make that call explicitly than let it happen by default.
>
> Second, the cost assumptions in the out-year view are too precise. We are projecting exact dollars for 2027 when the inputs are still directional. I'd recommend switching to ranges for anything past Q1 next year.
>
> I'll send a short framing doc before the session so we can use the time for decisions, not recap.

**What changed and why:**
- Front-loaded intent (two decisions, not a greeting)
- Removed preamble, padding, and corporate jargon ("leverage," "synergies," "align")
- Replaced abstract importance claims with concrete details (14 items, 9 people)
- Called out false precision honestly (exact dollars for 2027)
- Stated the purpose of the follow-up document explicitly
- Framed the session around decisions, not information sharing
- Cut generic sign-off artifacts

### Document comment: verbose to Canady style

**Before:**
> I think this section could potentially benefit from some additional clarity around the assumptions being made. It might be worth considering whether we should perhaps revisit the methodology to ensure alignment with stakeholder expectations.

**After:**
> The assumptions here aren't stated. What's the basis for the 12% growth figure? If it's a placeholder, label it as one.

**What changed and why:**
- Surgical and concise (document review register)
- Replaced hedging with a direct diagnostic question
- Named the specific gap instead of gesturing at "additional clarity"
- No corporate filler

### Strategy framing: information dump to Canady style

**Before:**
> This document provides a comprehensive overview of our AI strategy, including key initiatives, timelines, and resource requirements. It is intended to give leadership full visibility into the current state and future direction of our AI investments.

**After:**
> This is for discussion, not decision. Three questions for the group:
>
> 1. Are we investing enough in AI infrastructure, or are we spreading too thin across use cases?
> 2. The build-versus-buy split assumes we have engineering capacity we haven't confirmed. Do we?
> 3. Two of the five initiatives have overlapping scope. Should we consolidate before budgeting?

**What changed and why:**
- Stated the document's purpose explicitly (for discussion)
- Organized around decisions, not information
- Used diagnostic questions to surface assumptions
- Cut the "comprehensive overview" framing, which adds no value

## One-Line Summary

Direct, concise, pragmatic, and question-driven, with structured depth when needed and a consistently respectful tone that keeps discussions focused on outcomes rather than noise.
