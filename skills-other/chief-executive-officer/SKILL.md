# Chief Executive Officer

## Role Context
The CEO leads the direct-to-consumer life insurance operation, accountable for the full P&L, growth strategy, product portfolio, operating model, regulatory standing, and organizational health of the business. The CEO operates within the strategic and financial oversight of the parent company while maintaining day-to-day operational autonomy.

## Core Competencies

### Enterprise Strategy & Vision
- Define the 3–5 year strategic plan covering growth targets, product portfolio evolution, channel mix, and technology investment priorities
- Set the competitive positioning of the organization within the DTC life insurance market: value proposition, price positioning, brand differentiation
- Evaluate adjacencies and growth opportunities: new product lines (supplemental health, accidental death), new distribution channels (partnership, worksite), new demographic segments
- Direct annual strategic planning cycle with executive team including market analysis, capability investment prioritization, and financial target-setting
- Communicate strategic vision to parent company leadership, board of directors, and internal organization

### P&L Ownership & Financial Performance
- Own the income statement: gross premium written, net premium income, insurance benefits, operating expenses, marketing spend, and pre-tax operating income
- Set annual financial targets in partnership with CFO and Chief Actuary: volume (policies issued), revenue (premium), expense ratios, and profitability
- Review and approve annual budget and multi-year financial plan
- Monitor monthly and quarterly financial performance: variance from budget, corrective actions, forecast updates to parent company
- Make major resource allocation decisions: marketing investment by channel, technology capital investments, headcount plans
- Approve all investments above defined financial thresholds; define delegation of authority schedule

### Growth Strategy — Direct-to-Consumer Life Insurance
- Direct overall customer acquisition strategy across Digital (paid search, social, display, email) and Print (direct mail) channels
- Set volume growth targets by product and channel; hold CMO accountable to cost-per-issued-policy (CPIP) targets
- Define product development priorities: which products to introduce, modify, or retire based on competitive positioning, actuarial performance, and consumer demand
- Evaluate channel innovation: digital direct, embedded insurance partnerships, employer/worksite distribution, affinity programs
- Partner with CMO on brand strategy: positioning the organization as an accessible, trustworthy, digitally convenient life insurance option backed by the parent company's brand heritage

### Organizational Leadership & Culture
- Build and lead the executive team: CEO direct reports span CMO, CFO, CTO/CIO, Chief Actuary, COO, and CRO
- Set organizational culture and values: customer focus, data-driven decision-making, operational excellence, compliance integrity
- Define organizational structure: functional model, team sizing, insource vs. outsource decisions for key capabilities
- Drive talent strategy: attract, retain, and develop senior talent; set compensation philosophy aligned to Total Rewards framework
- Cascade strategic priorities to management team; hold executives accountable through performance management framework

### Parent/Holding Company Relationship
- Serve as the primary interface between the division and parent company executive leadership
- Manage capital and dividend policy with the parent company: surplus management, capital contributions, reinsurance agreements
- Represent the division in enterprise-wide initiatives: shared services, data governance, technology platforms, brand standards
- Report financial and operational performance to parent company on monthly and quarterly cycles
- Navigate parent/subsidiary tension between divisional autonomy and enterprise standards

### Regulatory & Industry Relationships
- Ensure the organization maintains good standing with state insurance departments across all licensed states: no uncured consent orders, no suspended licenses
- Engage directly with state insurance regulators for major regulatory matters: new product filings, examination findings, legal department escalations
- Represent the organization with insurance industry associations (e.g., ACLI, LIDMA, state associations relevant to DTC marketing)
- Oversee major regulatory responses and corrective action plans; ensure Lead Compliance Officer has executive support for remediation
- Champion ethical business practices: fair treatment of consumers, transparent marketing, compliant underwriting

### Major Investment & Vendor Decisions
- Approve major technology platform investments: policy administration system, enterprise data platform, core MarTech stack
- Evaluate build vs. buy decisions for strategic capabilities with CTO/CIO; apply long-term competitive advantage lens
- Approve strategic partnerships: embedded insurance distributors, affinity partners, lead generation partners, technology vendors
- Oversee major contract negotiations above defined thresholds; escalate to parent company for enterprise-level procurement where applicable

## CEO Decision Framework

The CEO prioritizes decisions using the following hierarchy:
1. **Consumer protection and regulatory compliance**: Non-negotiable; no growth objective overrides compliance obligations
2. **Financial solvency**: RBC and surplus management always protected; no aggressive growth that impairs capital
3. **Brand integrity**: The organization's actions must align with parent company brand standards and long-term reputation
4. **Long-term value creation**: Sustainable, profitable growth over short-term volume at the expense of profitability
5. **Operational excellence**: Disciplined execution; hold all functions accountable to their performance targets

## Collaboration Interfaces

- **CMO**: Marketing strategy, channel investment, brand, and customer acquisition performance
- **CFO**: Financial performance, budget, capital management, and statutory reporting
- **Chief Actuary**: Product pricing, reserving, mortality, and reinsurance strategy
- **CTO/CIO**: Technology strategy, platform investments, digital channel reliability
- **COO**: Operational execution, process efficiency, vendor management, and service delivery
- **CRO**: Enterprise risk framework, emerging risks, and regulatory risk posture
- **Lead Compliance Officer**: Regulatory compliance program health, examination management
- **NYL Parent Leadership**: Strategy alignment, capital, brand governance, enterprise programs

## CEO Presentation Design — Technology & Transformation Investments

Use this framework whenever building a presentation for or about the CEO on a major technology investment, capability build, or strategic initiative. This framework was validated on the PAS/Personalization at Scale CLevel v6 deck (May 2026, 60,496 bytes) and produced a materially stronger CEO narrative than the prior version.

### The Proven 5-Page Story Arc

Every complex technology or transformation investment should be structured in this sequence. Pages map to the CEO's natural decision flow: understand the problem → trust the diagnosis → evaluate the response → verify the economics → make a decision.

| Page | Nav Label | Badge Color | Purpose | CEO Question It Answers |
|------|-----------|-------------|---------|------------------------|
| 1 | The Gap | Amber / The Situation | Establish the problem at the customer experience level, not the technology level. Show the before/after in specific, named scenarios. Anchor to external research. | "Why does this matter and why now?" |
| 2 | External Findings | Violet / [Consultant] Assessment | Present the external validator's findings verbatim — in their words, not yours. Show the framework they used, their explicit timing/sequencing, and any open decisions they flagged. | "Who else agrees and what specifically did they find?" |
| 3 | Our Response | Blue / Our Response | Show the options (at least 2, clearly labeled). Present the recommended option's control mechanism (gates, checkpoints, decision criteria). Show capability build by year. Name the operating model shift explicitly. | "What exactly are we committing to and what do we control?" |
| 4 | Financial Case | Green / Financial Case | Lead with the scale of the opportunity (baseline revenue). Show investment, return, scenario range, and what drives the Year 2/Year 3 step-up. Reference external CBA as a floor. Name the governance finding. | "Does the math work, and what makes Year 2/3 better than Year 1?" |
| 5 | The Ask | Blue / The Ask | Limit to 3 decisions. Each decision must be binary, have IBM/external attribution where applicable, and name the consequence of deferral. Include a 90-day timeline and a "Day 1 reality" callout. | "What do you need from me right now?" |

### Page 1 Design — The Gap

**Lead with the headline, not the background.** The opening sentence should name the dormant opportunity in dollar or customer terms, not describe the history of the problem.

**Before/After format is mandatory for technology investments.** Generic framing ("we lack real-time capabilities") does not move a CEO. Concrete scenario-level framing does. Use `.ba-grid` split layout.

**Before/After design rules:**
- Name specific internal systems by name in each scenario (e.g., "Paymentus," "Phoenix," "AbiliTec," "SageMaker") — this proves the current state is known and the future state is engineered, not aspirational
- Each "before" scenario must name a specific failure mode: wrong channel, wrong timing, no attribution, no suppression
- Each "after" scenario must name a specific mechanism: which system fires what event, within what latency, with what compliance check
- Use 3–4 scenarios minimum: one retention/lapse, one acquisition/journey abandon, one life event, one attribution/measurement scenario

**Research anchoring on Page 1:**
- Anchor the structural nature of the problem to external research (not internal data alone)
- Use the `.research-badge` visual element to make citations visually distinct
- At least one citation should address measurement/governance failure (not just personalization demand) — this reframes the investment as operational discipline, not just a technology upgrade

**What to include on Page 1 — right side:**
- A "What we already have" checklist (in-contract, in-production assets) — this pre-empts the "why do we need more money" question and establishes that the program builds on existing assets, not a greenfield rip-and-replace

---

### Page 2 Design — External Findings

**This page earns the CEO's trust. It must be in the external validator's words, not yours.**

Core rule: Every major claim on this page must be directly attributed to the external party. Use the verbatim pull quote block (`.pull-quote`, dark navy background) as the anchor. The pull quote should be the validator's clearest statement of the problem/opportunity — not a complimentary quote about your team.

**What must appear on Page 2:**
1. **Verbatim pull quote** — the consultant's exact language describing the current-state problem
2. **The consultant's framework** — their named pillars, dimensions, or capability model (not your interpretation of it)
3. **Timing/sequencing** — the consultant's explicit sequencing language, verbatim. If they said "Start Now," show that quote. Attribution to the specific deliverable (date, document name) is required.
4. **Open decisions** — any items the consultant flagged as unresolved. Show these with a `.cross-list` style and connect them to your response — the point is that your program resolves them, theirs does not.
5. **Principles or architecture standards** — if the consultant provided design principles, show all of them (use a 4-column or 2-column grid). Show at the bottom that your recommended option addresses all principles.
6. **Signal/data backlog** (for data/AI investments) — show the specific data elements the consultant identified as underutilized. Organized by type (life events, behavioral, financial, model enhancements).
7. **Consultant's financial case** — show their benefit estimate, labeled as "draft" or "floor estimate" if they flagged it that way. This makes your internal model look moderate by comparison.

**A key design principle:** "IBM/[consultant name] found it — we are responding to it" is far more defensible than "we believe we need it." The CEO hears this difference. Design the page to establish that distinction clearly.

---

### Page 3 Design — Our Response

**Show at least two options, clearly labeled, with asymmetric comparison.** The CEO needs to feel the choice, not just approve the recommendation. Options must differ on a meaningful dimension — not scope, but control mechanism or risk profile.

**Gate/checkpoint design (for multi-year investments):**
- Show all gates, not a summary. Every gate must have: a gate number, the quarter it occurs, the specific binary criterion (not a description of a meeting), and the consequence of not passing
- The key gates to feature prominently: (a) the CFO budget-hold gate for Year 2 capital, (b) the data quality gate before a significant new platform is funded, (c) the technology parallel-run gate before any system cutover
- Gate language should be measurement-based: "null rate ≤2%," "match rate ≥75%," "suppression parity ≥99%" — not "evaluation complete" or "assessment submitted"

**Capability-by-year table format:**
- Three columns: Year 1, Year 2, Year 3
- Each year uses a color-coded header (navy/navy-2/navy-3 with blue/violet/green text respectively)
- Items within each year should name specific platforms or capabilities — not generic phrases like "enhanced personalization"

**Operating model shift table (mandatory for transformation investments):**
- This is the behavioral change section — the hardest part of the investment that is most often omitted
- Format: Today vs. Year 3across 5 dimensions: Decision Unit, Planning Horizon, Execution Model, Success Metric, Budget Logic
- Without this table, the CEO cannot understand why this is an organizational transformation, not just a technology purchase

### Page 4 Design — Financial Case

**Lead with the baseline revenue number, not the investment number.** The CEO must see the scale of the opportunity before seeing the cost.

**Stat card row format (4 cards):**
1. Channel baseline revenue ($XYZ.XM) — the pool of premium the program is protecting and optimizing
2. Net Year 1 investment ($X.XM after offset) — after savings credits, not gross
3. Net ROI ratio (X–Yx) or payback period
4. 3-year net benefit ($XX.XM cumulative)

**The "what drives Year 2/Year 3 step-up" table is non-negotiable.** If this table is absent, the CEO will ask why Year 2/3 are better than Year 1, and no one in the room will have a credible answer. Format: capability name | what becomes available at that capability level | benefit mechanism. Connect CDP → real-time segmentation → lapse reduction. Connect Journey Orchestration → cross-channel attribution → media efficiency.

**External CBA reference:** Show the external consultant's benefit estimate alongside the internal model. Position the internal model as moderate relative to the consultant's floor. This inverts the typical dynamic where internal projections look optimistic.

**Governance finding:** Always include the McKinsey/Gartner or equivalent finding that connects governance discipline to ROI realization. The CEO insight is: "The technology is not the risk — the governance is." Frame accordingly.

---

### Page 5 Design — The Ask

**Limit to exactly 3 decisions. Never more.**

A CEO presentation that ends with a long list of asks is a presentation that asks the CEO to do the presenter's job. Three is the limit. Each decision must:
- Be binary (approve/decline or yes/no with a defined consequence)
- Have a named owner for execution (not just "the team")
- Have IBM/external attribution if the decision was flagged by the external validator
- Name the consequence of deferral explicitly — not implied

**90-day timeline format:**
- Three stages: Month 1 (governance + vendor), Months 2–3 (data and content foundation), Month 3 (first signal activations)
- The first stage should always be governance — this signals to the CEO that money is not spent until the control structure is in place

**Day 1 green callout box:**
- Shows what is already authorized and happening — no new decision required
- Anchors the fact that executable work does not wait for the full program budget to be approved
- Critical for large multi-year investments where the CEO may feel the program is too large to approve in one meeting

**IBM/external alignment info-box:**
- Recap what the external validator said will happen next (their stated next steps, verbatim)
- Show alignment between their stated next steps and the first 90 days of the program
- This closes the loop: "IBM recommended it, IBM said 'Start Now,' and here is what we're starting with"

### Sourcing Principles for CEO Presentations

Every factual claim in a CEO presentation should be traceable to one of three source tiers:

| Tier | Examples | How to Display |
|------|---------|----------------|
| T1 — External Consultant (Engaged) | IBM, McKinsey, Deloitte, Gartner — engaged by the company | Verbatim pull quote block. Named document. Date. "Quoted directly" attribution. |
| T2 — Published Research (Third Party) | Adobe Consumer Study 2026, McKinsey CMO Survey 2025, Gartner Martech Utilization 2025 | `.research-badge` violet pill. Source name. Year. Specific statistic. |
| T3 — Internal Data (Company-Owned) | Company P&L, channel revenue, policy counts, model performance | Table or stat card with a warn-box flag if numbers are pending confirmation against internal reporting systems. |

**Never mix tiers without attribution.** If a number comes from IBM's CBA, label it "IBM draft CBA, WIP." If it comes from the internal financial model, label it accordingly. If a published statistic is being used as context (not as a financial input), a `.research-badge` citation is sufficient.

### What to Exclude from a CEO Presentation

The following categories should be excluded or moved to an appendix. Their presence signals the presentation was designed for an architect, not a CEO:

- System architecture diagrams (swimlanes, data flow, integration topology) — link to the Arch deck instead
- Vendor product comparison matrices — include vendor selection rationale but not feature-by-feature tables
- Gate exit criteria in technical detail (null rates, API latency specs) — show the gate's business purpose; move technical thresholds to the gate register
- Data Vault 2.0 modeling methodology — foundational but not a CEO decision topic
- TCPA/CAN-SPAM compliance detail — present as "embedded in gate criteria"; do not expand
- Full IBM 12-principle definitions with long descriptions — use short 2-line descriptions in a grid, not paragraphs
